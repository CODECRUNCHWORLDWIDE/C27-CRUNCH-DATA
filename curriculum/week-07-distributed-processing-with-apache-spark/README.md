# Week 7 — Distributed Processing with Apache Spark

In Week 6 you stopped treating storage as an afterthought. You learned why Parquet's columnar layout lets a query read three columns out of a hundred without touching the other ninety-seven, you wrote the NYC taxi trips into a partitioned Parquet dataset, and you turned that dataset into an **Iceberg** table sitting on **MinIO** — a real object store speaking the S3 API on your laptop. By Friday of Week 6 you had a lakehouse: a pile of immutable Parquet files in object storage, plus a table format (Iceberg) that gives those files a schema, snapshots, hidden partitioning, and time travel, queryable by DuckDB in a single process. The whole thing ran in Docker and never left your machine. That is the substrate. Week 7 is about what happens when one machine — one DuckDB process, one Postgres backend, one Python script with a pandas DataFrame — is no longer enough, and you have to spread the computation across many cores, many machines, and many partitions at once. The tool for that, the one every data platform on earth eventually reaches for, is **Apache Spark** (Zaharia et al. 2016, *Communications of the ACM* 59:56; <https://spark.apache.org/>).

Spark's central idea is almost embarrassingly simple to state and genuinely hard to internalize: **a big computation is a graph of small computations on partitions of data, scheduled across a pool of workers.** Your program runs in a single process called the **driver**. The driver does not touch your data row by row. Instead it builds a *plan* — a directed acyclic graph (DAG) of transformations — and then ships the work out to a fleet of **executor** processes, each of which holds a slice of the data (a **partition**) in memory or on disk and runs **tasks** against its slice in parallel. The driver is the conductor; the executors are the orchestra; the partitions are the sheet music handed out one stand at a time. When you call `df.filter(...).groupBy(...).count()`, nothing computes. Spark records your intent. Only when you call an **action** — `.show()`, `.count()`, `.write.save(...)` — does the driver compile the recorded DAG into **stages**, break each stage into **tasks** (one task per partition), and dispatch those tasks to executors. This is **lazy evaluation**, and it is the reason Spark can look at your whole pipeline at once and optimize it before a single byte moves. The Apache Spark documentation calls this the *cluster mode overview* (<https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/cluster-overview.html>); read it twice.

The hinge of the entire week — the one concept that separates an engineer who *uses* Spark from one who *fights* Spark — is the **shuffle**. Some transformations are **narrow**: each output partition depends on exactly one input partition, so the work stays local to an executor and nothing crosses the network. `filter`, `select`, `withColumn`, and `map` are narrow. They are cheap, they pipeline together, and Spark fuses them into a single stage. Other transformations are **wide**: an output partition depends on *many* input partitions, because rows that share a key have to end up co-located before they can be aggregated or joined. `groupBy`, `join`, `distinct`, and `repartition` are wide. To make rows with the same key meet, Spark has to physically **redistribute every row across the network**, writing partition files to disk on the map side and pulling them across to the reduce side. That redistribution is the shuffle, and it is the single most expensive thing Spark does. The lecture title this week — **"The shuffle is the enemy"** — is not hyperbole. Almost every Spark performance problem you will ever debug is, at bottom, a shuffle problem: too much data shuffled, the wrong join strategy forcing an avoidable shuffle, or one key's worth of shuffled data landing on one unlucky task. Spark's *RDD Programming Guide* explains narrow vs wide dependencies precisely (<https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/rdd-programming-guide.html#shuffle-operations>); the *SQL Performance Tuning* page is where the cures live (<https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/sql-performance-tuning.html>).

When a shuffle does happen, the question becomes *how* the rows are made to meet, and that is the **join strategy** decision. If one side of a join is small — a dimension table of payment types, a lookup of vendor names, a calendar — Spark can **broadcast** it: ship the entire small table to every executor and do the join locally, with **no shuffle at all**. This is the **broadcast hash join**, and it is the single biggest lever you have. Spark will do it automatically when it believes one side is under `spark.sql.autoBroadcastJoinThreshold` (10 MB by default), and you can force it with the `broadcast()` hint from `pyspark.sql.functions`. When both sides are large, Spark falls back to the **sort-merge join**: shuffle both sides by the join key, sort each partition, and merge. Correct, scalable, and expensive — two shuffles and two sorts. Knowing which join you got, and why, is a matter of reading the **physical plan** with `df.explain(mode="formatted")` and looking for `BroadcastHashJoin` versus `SortMergeJoin` in the output. The Spark SQL guide documents join hints (<https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/sql-ref-syntax-qry-select-hints.html>); we will read plans until that output is as legible to you as a stack trace.

Then there is **skew**, the failure mode that humbles everyone eventually. Spark divides work into tasks of roughly equal *count*, but not equal *cost*. If one join key holds 40% of the rows — and in the NYC taxi data, vendor `2` and a single dominant pickup zone really do — then the one task that gets that key does ten times the work of its neighbors. The other 199 tasks finish in seconds; that one task, the **straggler**, runs for minutes while the whole stage waits for it. The cluster is 99% idle and the job is "slow." You cannot fix this by adding cores. You fix it by reading the **Spark UI** — specifically the *Stages* tab, where the task-duration distribution screams the moment one task's max is 50× the median — and then by either **broadcasting** the small side to skip the shuffle, **salting** the hot key (append a random suffix so it spreads across many tasks), or turning on **Adaptive Query Execution**'s automatic skew-join handling (`spark.sql.adaptive.enabled` and `spark.sql.adaptive.skewJoin.enabled`, both documented at <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/sql-performance-tuning.html#adaptive-query-execution>). The *Web UI* page (<https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/web-ui.html>) is your stethoscope; learning to read it is half of being good at Spark.

And the honest counterweight to all of this: **Spark is not always the answer.** Spark exists to spread a computation that does not fit on one machine across many machines. But machines got big. A single modern laptop has 8–16 cores and 32–64 GB of RAM; a single cloud instance has hundreds of cores and terabytes of RAM. **DuckDB** — the in-process analytical engine you used in Week 6 — will out-run Spark on any dataset that fits comfortably on one big machine, because it pays none of the coordination tax: no driver/executor split, no JVM serialization, no shuffle-to-disk, no network. Spark's overhead is the price of *distribution*; if you do not need distribution, you should not pay it. The rule of thumb you will earn by Friday: **if the working set fits on one machine's memory with room to spare, use DuckDB; if it does not, or if you genuinely need to scale out across a cluster, use Spark.** This week's lab makes you *feel* that boundary: you will rebuild Week 6's dimensional mart twice — once as a Spark job over a large multi-month NYC taxi dataset on the Iceberg lakehouse, once as the DuckDB single-node version — and put the two wall-clock times next to each other in a `PERF.md`. Sometimes Spark wins. Often, at laptop scale, DuckDB wins. The skill is knowing which, and being able to defend it.

By Friday you will be able to: stand up Spark in Docker against your existing MinIO + Iceberg lakehouse; write a PySpark **DataFrame** job that reads Iceberg tables, joins them, aggregates them, and writes a new mart table back; read a physical plan and name every operator in it; deliberately inject a skewed join, watch it stall in the Spark UI, identify the straggler task, and fix it two different ways (broadcast and salting) with a measured before/after; and — the meta-skill — argue from numbers whether a given job belongs on Spark at all. The dataset is the real **NYC TLC trip-record data** (<https://www.nyc.gov/site/tlc/about/tlc-trip-record-data.page>), the same yellow-taxi Parquet you met in Week 6, scaled up to multiple months so that the distributed machinery has something worth distributing.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

- **Distinguish** transformations from actions and **narrow** dependencies from **wide** dependencies, and predict from a chain of DataFrame calls exactly where a stage boundary (a shuffle) will fall. <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/rdd-programming-guide.html#transformations>
- **Create** a `SparkSession` in a Docker container with the Iceberg extensions and S3A configuration wired to MinIO, and confirm it can read and write a catalog table. <https://iceberg.apache.org/docs/latest/spark-getting-started/>
- **Write** a PySpark DataFrame job over lakehouse tables — `spark.read`, `filter`, `select`, `withColumn`, `join`, `groupBy`, `agg`, `write` — that rebuilds a dimensional mart, and reason about its partitioning with `df.rdd.getNumPartitions()` and `spark.sql.shuffle.partitions`. <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/sql-programming-guide.html>
- **Read** a physical plan from `df.explain(mode="formatted")` and identify `Exchange` (shuffle), `BroadcastHashJoin`, `SortMergeJoin`, `HashAggregate`, and `AdaptiveSparkPlan` nodes, and say which transformation in your code produced each. <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/sql-ref-syntax-qry-explain.html>
- **Tune** the shuffle by setting `spark.sql.shuffle.partitions` appropriately for a dataset size, and explain why the 200 default is wrong for both very small and very large jobs. <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/sql-performance-tuning.html#other-configuration-options>
- **Choose** between a broadcast hash join and a sort-merge join, justify the choice from the relative sizes of the two inputs, and force a broadcast with the `broadcast()` hint when Spark's automatic threshold (`spark.sql.autoBroadcastJoinThreshold`) does not fire. <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/sql-performance-tuning.html#join-strategy-hints>
- **Diagnose** data skew by reading the *Stages* tab of the Spark UI — comparing the max task duration and shuffle-read size against the median — and name the one key that is hot. <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/web-ui.html#stages-tab>
- **Fix** a skewed join three ways — broadcasting the small side, **salting** the hot key, and enabling AQE skew-join handling (`spark.sql.adaptive.skewJoin.enabled`) — and measure the wall-clock improvement of each. <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/sql-performance-tuning.html#optimizing-skew-join>
- **Enable** Adaptive Query Execution (`spark.sql.adaptive.enabled`) and describe its three runtime optimizations — coalescing shuffle partitions, switching join strategies, and splitting skewed partitions — and verify in the plan that `AdaptiveSparkPlan isFinalPlan=true` appears. <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/sql-performance-tuning.html#adaptive-query-execution>
- **Compare** the Spark version of the mart against the DuckDB single-node version with a measured wall-clock benchmark, and write a defensible rule for when distributed compute pays for its overhead and when it does not. <https://duckdb.org/docs/stable/guides/performance/overview.html>
- **Read** the Spark UI's *Jobs*, *Stages*, *SQL/DataFrame*, and *Executors* tabs well enough to find a shuffle, a straggler task, and a memory-pressured executor in a job you did not write. <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/web-ui.html>

## Topics covered

- **Why distributed compute exists.** The boundary where one machine stops being enough: data that does not fit in RAM, computation that does not finish in the time you have, the difference between scaling *up* (a bigger machine) and scaling *out* (more machines). Amdahl's law and the coordination tax of distribution. Reference: Zaharia et al. 2016, "Apache Spark: A Unified Engine for Big Data Processing," *Communications of the ACM* 59:56 (<https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/2934664>).
- **The Spark execution model.** The **driver** runs your program and holds the `SparkSession`/`SparkContext`. The **cluster manager** (local, standalone, YARN, Kubernetes, Mesos) grants resources. **Executors** are JVM processes that hold data partitions and run tasks. A **job** is triggered by one action; the **DAG scheduler** splits the job into **stages** at shuffle boundaries; each stage is a set of **tasks**, one per partition. Reference: cluster-mode overview, <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/cluster-overview.html>.
- **Lazy evaluation and the DAG.** Transformations build a lineage graph and compute nothing; actions trigger execution. Spark uses the recorded DAG to fuse narrow transformations, prune columns and partitions, and pick join strategies before any data moves. Reference: RDD Programming Guide, <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/rdd-programming-guide.html>.
- **The DataFrame API.** Structured, schema-carrying, columnar, optimized by the **Catalyst** optimizer and executed via **Tungsten** code generation. `read`, `select`, `filter`, `withColumn`, `join`, `groupBy`, `agg`, `orderBy`, `write`. Why DataFrames beat raw RDDs for almost everything. Reference: Spark SQL/DataFrame guide, <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/sql-programming-guide.html>.
- **Partitions and parallelism.** A partition is the unit of parallelism: one task processes one partition. How partition count is set on read (file/block layout), how it changes on shuffle (`spark.sql.shuffle.partitions`, default 200), and how `repartition` (full shuffle) differs from `coalesce` (no shuffle, only merges). Reference: SQL performance tuning, <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/sql-performance-tuning.html>.
- **Narrow vs wide transformations and the shuffle.** Narrow = one input partition per output partition, stays local. Wide = many input partitions per output partition, forces a network redistribution. The shuffle writes map-side files to disk, sorts/serializes, and pulls them reduce-side. Why a shuffle is the most expensive operation Spark performs. Reference: <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/rdd-programming-guide.html#shuffle-operations>.
- **Join strategies.** **Broadcast hash join** (ship the small side everywhere, no shuffle), **sort-merge join** (shuffle and sort both sides), **shuffle hash join** (shuffle both, hash the smaller). The `spark.sql.autoBroadcastJoinThreshold` (10 MB default), the `broadcast()` hint, and how to force or forbid each strategy. Reference: join hints, <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/sql-ref-syntax-qry-select-hints.html>.
- **Data skew.** When one key holds a disproportionate share of rows, the task that gets it becomes a straggler and stalls the stage. Detecting skew from the Spark UI's task-duration distribution. Fixing it with broadcast, salting, or AQE skew-join handling. Reference: <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/sql-performance-tuning.html#optimizing-skew-join>.
- **Adaptive Query Execution (AQE).** Re-optimization at runtime using actual shuffle statistics: coalescing small shuffle partitions, dynamically switching sort-merge to broadcast when a side turns out to be small, and splitting skewed partitions. `spark.sql.adaptive.enabled` (on by default since 3.2). Reference: <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/sql-performance-tuning.html#adaptive-query-execution>.
- **The Spark UI.** *Jobs* (one row per action, the timeline), *Stages* (task-duration and shuffle-size distributions — where skew shows), *SQL/DataFrame* (the per-query plan with row counts and durations on each node), *Executors* (per-executor cores, memory, GC time, shuffle read/write). Reading a job you did not write. Reference: <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/web-ui.html>.
- **Reading a physical plan.** `df.explain(mode="formatted")`. The bottom-up read order, the meaning of `Exchange hashpartitioning`, `BroadcastExchange`, `*(n)` whole-stage codegen markers, `Filter`/`Project` pushdown, and `AdaptiveSparkPlan`. Reference: EXPLAIN, <https://spark.apache.org/docs/latest/sql-ref-syntax-qry-explain.html>.
- **Spark vs DuckDB.** The single-big-machine alternative. When the coordination tax of distribution is not worth paying, and how to decide from a measured benchmark rather than from habit. Reference: DuckDB performance guide, <https://duckdb.org/docs/stable/guides/performance/overview.html>.

## A note on tone

C27 is written in **engineer's-logbook voice**. We pin versions ("Spark 3.5.3, Iceberg 1.6.1, PySpark 3.5.3, DuckDB 1.1.x"). We cite operations by what the plan calls them ("the `Exchange hashpartitioning(VendorID, 200)` node is the shuffle that `groupBy('VendorID')` forced"). We say "the skewed stage had 200 tasks; 199 finished in under 4 s, one ran for 92 s with 1.8 GB of shuffle read — that single straggler held vendor 2, which is 41% of the rows; broadcasting the 6-row payment-type dimension cut the stage from 94 s to 7 s" — not "the job was slow and we made it faster." A benchmark is a number with a unit and a baseline. If your `PERF.md` says "Spark is fast" without a wall-clock and a comparison, you have not written one yet.

## A note on data size

Spark's machinery only earns its keep when there is enough data to distribute. The lab data scales accordingly:

- **One month** of NYC yellow-taxi Parquet (the Week 6 starting point) is **~50 MB** and ~3 million rows — small enough that DuckDB beats Spark handily, and a useful *baseline* to feel the overhead.
- **A full year** (12 monthly Parquet files) is **~600 MB** and ~38 million rows — where Spark starts to make sense on a laptop and where the shuffle and skew effects become visible in the UI.
- The **mart output** (a star schema: one fact table plus a handful of dimensions, aggregated to daily/zone/vendor grain) is **a few MB** — tiny, because aggregation collapses the rows.
- Spark's **shuffle spill** and **warehouse** directories can balloon to several GB during a wide join on the year of data; point them at a disk with room (`spark.local.dir`) and clean them between runs.

Commit your job scripts, the `PERF.md`, the mart schema, and the Spark-UI screenshots; gitignore the raw taxi Parquet, the Spark warehouse, and the shuffle spill. For genuinely large-scale work (hundreds of GB to TB), the same code runs unchanged on a real cluster — that is the whole point of the DataFrame abstraction.
